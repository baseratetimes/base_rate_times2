Russia is spending surprisingly little on its war on Ukraine...
• The estimated cost of the invasion stands at 5 trillion roubles ($67bn) annually, about 3% of Russia's GDP.
• This expenditure is considerably low compared to historical standards. For instance, during WWII, the USSR and the U.S. allocated 61% and 50% of their GDPs to war efforts, respectively.
• It would be challenging for Russia to increase war expenditure without directly impacting its citizens through inflation, debt, tax increases, or shifting public expenditure towards defense.
• Modern armed forces require fewer personnel, and their equipment is more accurate. As a result, spending less now compared to historical figures like in 1945 can still maintain a potent military force.

Counteroffensive
Ukraine facing tough fight, but the “big blow” is still yet to come...
• The current counteroffensive, launched on June 4th, spans 600 miles from northern Donetsk Oblast to central Zaporizhzhia Oblast, has been challenging due to complex terrain, weather, Russian preparedness, and minefields.
• Ukraine's Deputy Defense Minister, Hanna Maliar, explains the offensive is making more progress in the south where the enemy is on the defensive. Some areas, like Kupiansk and Lyman, see the enemy advancing while Ukraine is on the defensive.
• Maliar and Defense Minister Oleksii Reznikov urge patience, caution against assessing the effectiveness of military actions solely based on territories gained or settlements liberated. They stress that war progresses gradually amidst enemy resistance.
• Russian Defense Minister Sergei Shoigu claims the counteroffensive as a failure, stating that all Ukrainian attacks have been repelled.
